What is Merger Arbitrage?
◈ A 60-second primer for new readers
The Setup
When Company A announces it will acquire Company B for $50 per share, Company B's stock immediately jumps, but rarely all the way to $50. It might trade at $48.50. That $1.50 gap is the merger arbitrage spread.
The Trade
An arb trader buys Company B at $48.50 and waits for the deal to close. When it does, they receive $50 per share — a guaranteed $1.50 profit per share regardless of where the broader market goes. Pure deal-driven return.
The Risk
If the deal breaks, blocked by regulators, financing falls through, or the buyer walks, the stock crashes back to its pre-announcement price. That's why predicting deal outcomes is the entire game. That's what Meridian does.
◈ A Real Example · How to Read the Dashboard
A deal shows Spread: +4.2% and V3 Risk: Very Low. That means the stock is trading 4.2% below the deal price. If the deal closes, which Meridian's model gives high confidence, you pocket that 4.2% return in roughly 6 months. Annualized, that's over 8%. With a break price of $32.10 shown below, you also know exactly where the stock falls if the deal breaks before risking a single dollar.

Six-Factor Base Score
Base 50 · Normalized to 0–100 · Used as Secondary Tiebreaker in 8–12% Uncertain Zone

The V3 score starts at 50 and is adjusted by six factors. For deals under 8% spread, the score separates Very Low from Low risk. For deals in the 8-12% Uncertain Zone, score above 60 gives Medium, below 60 gives High. The score alone produced 88.7% accuracy. Combined with spread rules it reaches 93.7%.
