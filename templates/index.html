A 60-second primer

What is merger arbitrage?

When one company agrees to buy another, the target's stock usually trades a little below the agreed price until the deal actually closes. Merger arbitrage is the strategy of buying that gap and collecting the difference when the deal goes through. It is one of the oldest ways to earn a return that does not depend on the market going up or down. It depends on one thing: does the deal close.

How it works
Step one
The setup
Company A announces it will buy Company B for $50 a share. Company B's stock jumps on the news, but it rarely climbs all the way to $50. It might settle at $48.50. That $1.50 gap is the merger arbitrage spread.
Step two
The trade
You buy Company B at $48.50 and wait. When the deal closes, you receive the full $50 a share. That is a $1.50 gain, earned no matter what the rest of the market did while you waited. The return came from the deal, not the market.
The catch
The risk
If the deal falls apart, the stock drops back toward where it started before the announcement. That is the whole game: the reward is the spread, and the risk is the deal breaking. Judging whether a deal will close is the entire skill.
The spread explained

The spread is not random. It is the market's way of pricing how likely the deal is to close. A tiny spread means traders are confident the deal goes through, so there is little gap left to earn. A wide spread means traders have doubts, so they demand a bigger reward for taking the risk.

This is why the spread is really a probability in disguise. A stock trading close to its deal price is the market saying the deal is nearly certain. A stock trading far below it is the market saying it is worried. Reading that gap correctly, and knowing when the market is too worried or not worried enough, is where the opportunity lives.

Why deals break

Most announced deals do close. But the ones that fall apart usually break for one of a few reasons, and knowing them is how you judge risk before you ever put money in.

Regulators
Antitrust agencies like the DOJ or FTC can block a deal or demand changes if they think it hurts competition. This is the most common source of real risk.
Financing
The buyer needs the money to actually pay. If financing falls through, especially in a private equity deal, the deal can collapse.
Shareholders
The target's shareholders usually have to vote yes. If they think the price is too low, they can reject it.
The buyer walks
If something major changes at the target company, the buyer may have the right to walk away under the terms of the agreement.
Reading a deal on Meridian

Say a deal shows a spread of 4.2% and a risk rating of Very Low. That means the stock is trading 4.2% below the price the buyer agreed to pay. If the deal closes, and a Very Low rating means Meridian sees strong odds it will, you earn that 4.2% when it does. If that takes about six months, that is more than 8% on an annual basis. And because Meridian also shows you the break price, the level the stock would likely fall to if the deal collapsed, you know your downside before you risk a single dollar.

The key insight spread predicts outcome

The most important signal in merger arbitrage is the spread itself: the size of the gap correlates strongly with the odds a deal closes. Deals trading below 8% spread reflect market confidence in closure. Deals above 12% reflect elevated break risk. This spread-based classification is the primary rule, and the V3 score provides analytical context within each bucket.
